# Caught in the act

#### **The Crime** 
The **event** was one of the **most famous crimes** in recent British history. It **wasn't a murder** and was not successful. 
The **crime** took place at the **Millennium Dome** (now the O2 Arena) in London. The target was a collection of **diamonds** worth **£350 million**, including the Millennium Star

#### **Police Operation**
Police officers spent **months monitoring suspects** and preparing a **careful operation**. They managed to **catch the thieves** while they were actually **committing the crime**

#### **The Execution of the plan**
The thieves drove a **huge vehicle through the wall**. When they were breaking the glass display case, they were immediately **surrounded by police officers**

#### **The Criminals**
The gang leaders were sent to prison. One leader, Beton, had **broken the law many times**. He was a **small-time burglar** who had been involved in previous crimes like trying to **deal drugs** and **money laundering**

# A new beginning 

#### **Dillon arrest and trial**:
In 1981, 21-year-old American **Dillon** was **charged**. He went **on trial** and was **found guilty by a jury**. He was **sentenced to life in prison**. He spent nearly three decades being **punished for a crime he hadn't committed**.

#### **Conflicting Evidence** 
Dillon’s girlfriend initially claimed she had **witnessed** him standing next to the dead body. Another **witness** claimed she had seen Dillon near the scene wearing a T-shirt with blood on it. A trained dog matched Dillon with the killer's shirt.

#### **Pardon**
**Dillon’s** **girlfriend** later **changed her statement**, claiming the police had persuaded her to provide false evidence. Six years after his sentence, Dillon had been granted a **pardon by the police**.

#### **Exoneration**
**27 years** after his arrest, **New evidence**, specifically **DNA evidence**, was used for the first time to solve the crime, proving he was **innocent**.
Dillon was **released, a free man**. He was awarded **$1.25 million in compensation**.

# A different kind of police

#### **London (Before 1829)**
London in the early nineteenth century was **not a very safe place**. **Street crime**, **robbers**, and **thieves** were common, and there were **no police officers**

#### **The First Police Force**
In **1829**, government minister **Robert Peel** set up England’s first police force. This followed the example of Scotland and Ireland.

#### **Uniform**
The first officers were all men; women police officers did not exist until almost a hundred years later. They wore **distinctive blue uniforms** and **very tall hats**. The tall hats were used to prevent them from being mistaken for soldiers.

#### **New Policing Concept**
Robert Peel wanted his officers to be **trusted and respected by the public**. This concept is known as **'policing by consent'**.

#### **Police equipment**
Officers** were **not armed with pistols**. They carried **'truncheons'** (long pieces of wood) to use if necessary. They also carried **'rattles'** to call for help.

#### **Nicknames and Walking on the beat**
The new officers were sometimes called **'peelers'** (after Peel) and, later, **'bobbies'** (after Robert).
A **national force** was established in **1856**. The practice of **'patrolling the streets'** is known as **'walking the beat'** and is still common today, although many patrols are now done in cars.

# Victorian Crime
#### **Social Division**
British society in the nineteenth century was **divided into rich and poor**. The rich believed the poor should be made to work hard
#### **Workhouses**
Poor people, including children, were sent to **workhouses** where they had to **wear uniforms, eat disgusting food, and work very hard**.
#### **Child Crime**
Children could be charged with **serious crimes like murder**. In 1849, **more than 10,000 children** under 17 were in adult prisons.
#### **Punishments**
**Prison** was a common punishment. **Transportation** to Australia (1788 and 1868) was another punishment, where 165,000 criminals were sent. Convicts sent to Australia were often **forced to do hard labour**.
#### **Hard Labour**
Hard labour, like **making ropes or breaking stones**, was a popular UK punishment, based on the idea that people would learn to **do hard work** and avoid breaking the law.
